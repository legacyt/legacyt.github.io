19Feb.6 Denomination of Money

Thought

TLDR: The $300 and $1000 bills should be introduced next

It’s been a while since we had a new denomination. As someone who has had to buy a car and pay rent in cash, the $100 bill isn’t enough anymore. Is the government trying to phase out cash in favor of more traceable methods of payment? Maybe. Regardless, the issue I’m tackling here is what denomination(s) should be next.

The US suite of common money includes ten denominations of $0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.25, 1, 5, 10, 20, 50 and 100. Let me plot these on a log scale and find a trendline that describes them.

The figure above suggests we do have fairly well distributed denominations, though it’s a little crowded in the $5, 10, 20 region. 

Using that trendline, we can generate a set of denominations that are more evenly spread across the $0.01 to 100 range. Is this necessary? No, the existing distribution pretty good, but there is no harm in looking at what a potentially more efficient system would look like. I calculated those values and rounded them to the nearest one significant digit so it would be feasible to combine them easily into other value packages (like $17). With this I generated a “Best Fit” distribution with 13 denominations of $0.01, 0.04, 0.10, 0.30, 0.90, 3, 7, 20, 60, 200, 400, 1000, 4000, 10000. Of course, even rounding to one significant digit still leaves us with a bill system that is rather inefficient when it comes to combining them into other values. I’ll come back to that issue.

Another way to look at the problem is to look at the ratio between pairs of denominations. For instance, a $5 and $1 have a 5:1 ratio. It turns out the average ratio of adjacent common denominations is exactly 3:1. I suspect this ratio would hold in most other currencies as well (though it’s worth checking). 3:1 is likely an ideal balance between the number of types of bills we have to deal with and the number of coins/bills we have to carry. In general, higher ratios are less precise, requiring more to be carried, while lower ratios require us to deal with more denominations of money.

With that in mind, and accepting that we want to keep a base of 10, one option that comes to mind is to base a denomination set on 1 and 3. That would be close to that ideal ratio of 3:1 while also being easy to make change with. Similar to the previous set, I generated a set of 12 denominations spanning the range from $0.01 to $10000. The values are simply $0.01, 0.03, 0.10, 0.30, 1, 3, 10, 30, 100, 300, 1000, 3000, 10000. The average ratio of adjacent denominations in this set is 3.2. I’ve plotted these values on a log plot with the original trend line.

Looks fine.

When picking new denominations, the ability to make change for other values is critical. For instance, when I calculated the evenly spread denominations based on the trend line, the values prior to rounding (0.909285, 2.554109, 7.174287, etc) would have made it nearly impossible to mentally calculate change. Rounding those values to the nearest digit helps (0.9, 3, 7, etc), but I think it’s possible to quantify in a more meaningful way the ability of a denomination system to make change. We can take a set of numbers (1-9, 10-100 x10) calculate how many pieces of money we’d need to carry that precise value and then average across those in some way to find the cash carrying efficiency of the denomination system. I’ve gone through that exercise and included it below.

It appears that the average efficiency of the 1s and 3s system is the same as the current system for values between 1 and 100 while the best fit system is slightly less efficient. A fourth system in which we only used $1, 10 and 100 would have an average efficiency of 4.79. The way I averaged these is not ideal – we’d probably want to put more weight on the values that are more often carried or used in transactions if we were particularly concerned about it - but this method is sufficient for my purpose.

Taken together, I believe the 1s and 3s system for creating denomination values makes a lot of sense. It’s failing is that it doesn’t split the 10s into even subdigits – two 50s or ten 10s or five 20s for a 100. It would be three 30s and a 10 for a hundred. I don’t think that is fundamentally more difficult, though it is slightly less elegant. The elegance comes from being consistent across the entire value range so there are fewer combinations that would need to be learned. That said, I don’t expect America to replace their denomination set for a new set that is differently elegant. I do think they should adopt it in the upper range. Below is my hybrid recommendation for the future set of American denominations. Is this like switching to kilos for everything over 30 lbs? Maybe.

If this were followed, the next bill would be a $300 followed by a $1000 bill. The more traditional alternatives would be a $200 or $500 bill but those are both quite a ways off the ideal ratio of 3:1. There is no perfect solution, but I’m rooting for $300.
